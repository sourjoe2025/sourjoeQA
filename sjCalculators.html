Reference

Baking is most repeatable by weight, but recipes still mix cups, spoons, ounces, grams, millilitres, oven temperatures, and butter sticks. Use these as practical North American baking references. Ingredient cup weights are approximate because flour type, grind, packing, brand, and humidity all matter.

Baker's Percentage Guide

Baker's math expresses every ingredient relative to recipe flour. Flour is 100%; water, salt, leaven, seeds, and inclusions are percentages of that flour basis.

Baker's % = ingredient amount ÷ recipe flour amount × 100

Common Baker's Percentage Guide

Water:
60–85% typical bread range. Higher hydration usually means a looser dough and more open crumb potential.
Salt:
1.8–2.2% common lean-bread range. Salt affects flavour, gluten strength, and fermentation speed.
Leaven:
10–30% often used, depending on schedule, starter strength, and desired acidity.
Oil / Fat:
0–10% depending on style. Fat tenderizes crumb and can soften crust.
Sugar / Honey:
0–10%+ depending on enrichment. Sweeteners feed browning and tenderness.
Seeds / Inclusions:
5–30%+ depending on style. Soakers may be needed so inclusions do not rob water from the dough.

Developer Notes / Model Logic

percent = ingredientG ÷ flourG × 100 ingredientG = flourG × percent ÷ 100 flourG = ingredientG ÷ (percent ÷ 100) grams = ounces × 28.349523125 ounces = grams ÷ 28.349523125

Leaven Hydration Guide

Leaven StyleTypical HydrationCommon Use / Behaviour
Stiff leaven / stiff levain50–65%Firm, dough-like build. Often used when the baker wants slower ripening, more strength, or a different acid balance. Good for breads where a less-liquid preferment is easier to fold into formula math.
Lievito madre styleAbout 45–50%Very firm culture associated with enriched Italian baking traditions. Requires more maintenance and more deliberate mixing than a loose starter.
Medium leaven70–85%A compromise build: easier to mix than a stiff leaven, but less soupy than 100%. Useful when dough strength and timing need a middle ground.
Liquid leaven100%Equal flour and water by weight. Common, easy to maintain, easy to mix into dough, and straightforward for baker's-percentage math.
High-hydration leaven110–125%+Loose, batter-like build. Can ripen quickly and can contribute extensibility, but needs closer timing because food is consumed faster.

SourJoe note: hydration is not “better” or “worse” by itself. It changes handling, ripening schedule, acidity profile, and how easily the leaven blends into the final dough.

Advanced Model Constants

This first-pass empirical model exposes its constants so the calculator can be tuned later without hiding the logic. The default reference point is 23°C, 9.3% inoculation, 65% hydration, and 2% salt.

Current Formula

TemperatureRate = Q10 ^ ((DoughTempC - ReferenceTempC) / 10) BulkInoculationRate = (InoculationPct / ReferenceInoculationPct) ^ BulkInoculationExponent ProofInoculationRate = (InoculationPct / ReferenceInoculationPct) ^ ProofInoculationExponent SaltRate = 1 - SaltCoefficient × (SaltPct - ReferenceSaltPct) HydrationRate = 1 + HydrationCoefficient × (HydrationPct - ReferenceHydrationPct) BulkHours = ReferenceBulkHours ÷ (TemperatureRate × BulkInoculationRate × SaltRate × HydrationRate) ProofHours = ReferenceProofHours ÷ (TemperatureRate × ProofInoculationRate × SaltRate × HydrationRate) TotalHours = BulkHours + ProofHours

Model caution: this is not a fermentation law. It is an adjustable field model. Starter strength, flour mix, dough handling, and the baker's chosen “done” point can move the real times.

Water Mixing Formula

This calculator assumes the two water portions have the same heat capacity and that heat loss to the cup, bowl, air, thermometer, and spoon is small.

hotMass = totalMass × (targetTemp - regularTemp) ÷ (hotTemp - regularTemp) regularMass = totalMass - hotMass mixedTemp = (hotMass × hotTemp + regularMass × regularTemp) ÷ totalMass

Boiling Water Is Not Always 100°C / 212°F

At higher elevation, water boils below 100°C / 212°F. That is why the hot-water temperature is editable. Bakers at altitude can enter their local boiling temperature instead of pretending every kitchen is at sea level.

Practical Baking Notes

  • Warm the measuring cup or mixing bowl when precision matters; cold vessels steal heat.
  • Measure by weight, not volume, for repeatable dough temperature work.
  • Stir the mixed water before measuring final temperature.
  • If the target temperature is outside the hot/regular water range, the calculator will warn you because the mix cannot reach it with only those two waters.
